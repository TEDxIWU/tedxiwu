Nicole Jovicevich '15

Why do I want to die? The transformative process of honoring your truth

Nicole Jovicevic is a career coach and life strategist who helps people tap into their greatest strengths, decide who they want to be, and take dynamic action to get there NOW. In addition to her business, she's a career coach at a nonprofit, a LinkedIn Local Chicago networking event host, a LinkedIn and resume fanatic, an amateur swing/blues/hop hop dancer, and a lover of watching Vine compilations and Netflix. She spends most of her free time with her boyfriend Kevin, mom, two cats, and close friends and is quite happy with that.

To learn more, visit https://nicolejconsulting.com or join her Facebook group The Massive Action Network with Nicole J.

What does "becoming human" mean to Nicole? To embrace the incredibly powerful and creative aspects of ourselves along with the weak and needy. To accept the impermanence and importance of everything we create and become. To acknowledge, honor, and follow the greater, intangible aspects of our inner knowing. To feel the joyful, expectant bliss of unfolding, blossoming, and becoming the vision we hold of ourselves. But Kevin says it's being birthed by people. We balance each other out.

Dr. Wendy Kooken

Right in Front of Our Eyes: Seeing People Who are Homeless

Dr. Wendy C. Kooken received an A.A.S. in nursing at Illinois Central College in 1985. She earned a B.S.N. in 1999 and a M.S.N. in Nursing Administration in 2002 from Bradley University. Her Ph.D. and post doctoral fellowship were completed at Indiana University in 2008 and 2011, respectively. Prior to joining the faculty at Illinois Wesleyan University in 2012, Dr. Kooken taught for Bradley University in Peoria, Western Illinois University in Macomb, Indiana University in Indianapolis, IN, and for Kansas University Medical Center. Her nursing positions have been diverse and include post anesthesia care at Pekin Hospital, tissue services coordinator for American Red Cross and American Tissue Services Foundation, and staff nurse at Crittenton Care and Counseling in Peoria. She volunteers as a 4H leader, loves her students and helping others find their passion. Her own passions include teaching, reading, embroidery/crewel, horseback riding, and most of all, her family.

What does "becoming human" mean to Dr. Kooken? Becoming comfortable with limitations and imperfections in ourselves and others.

Cindi Perrine '88

How Stories Can Make Us Human

Cindi graduated from IWU in 1988. She and her husband (Peter '86) married that same year and she started graduate work at the University of Illinois. After earning her Master of Science degree in Psychology, she began work at Accenture (formerly Andersen Consulting). She has worked there for 28 years in the areas of learning, design, and innovation. She has always been fascinated by the impact of technology on the human experience. Cindi is currently a certified storytelling coach and trainer within Accenture and leads a team that uses storytelling and design thinking to explore the future of technology with clients.

Personally, Cindi has three children who are all attending college. She and Peter are still married (happily!) and they live in Wheaton, Illinois. Cindi and Pete are long-term members of First United Methodist Church in Glen Ellyn, Illinois.

What does "becoming human" mean to Cindi? Becoming human means that we rest in our limitations and at the same time, realize that what we have to bring to the table is incredibly valuable and that we shouldn't shrink or hide from it. It's accepting our need for one another, acknowledging the greatness that's inside of us to give, and using our strengths to serve others. It's saying, "Yes, I'm only one human, but this one human is doing to make a difference for somebody."

Isaac Simmons '20

Bridging Thought: My Idea to Yours

From small town Illinois, Isaac Simmons is a Junior Business Management and Religious Studies double major at Illinois Wesleyan University (I.W.U.). On campus, Isaac is employed both as a Residential Community Advisor and as a Multifaith Ambassador. Off campus, Isaac serves as an advisor for the Advocate BroMenn Medical Center Delegate Church Association D.E.C., as an Advisor for Engaged Learning and Community Partnership at the I.W.U. Action Research Center (A.R.C.), and he serves as a member of the West Bloomington Economic Development Committee. In his time at I.W.U., Isaac has furthermore interned with the Downtown Bloomington Association and Marcfirst. Over the summer of 2018 Isaac researched and designed the W.B.R.P. SupplySuccess MicroLoan Program through the A.R.C. Westside Entrepreneurial Research Fellowship.

What does "becoming human" mean to Isaac? For me, becoming human is a constant everyday action. As college students, we are pushed and pushed to produce near perfect papers and we are expected remember minute details of texts, all the while, we are supposed to be preparing for our life after undergrad. So often, we forget that we too are human. We forget that it is okay to drop the ball or to fail at something. Becoming human means that when someone asks you, "How have you been?", it is perfectly acceptable to respond truthfully and not with the expected, "I've been busy!". Becoming human is an act of acknowledging and honoring my flaws and failures.

Amanda Wesche '20

Excellent Mistakes: Failure Doesn't Need to be a Learning Moment

Amanda is a junior at Illinois Wesleyan University studying sociology and gender. She grew up in Kansas City, Missouri with five older brothers and parents who taught her it's okay to make mistakes. She currently works as a residential community advisor and multifaith ambassador. Amanda is passionate about working with college-aged students and encouraging people to take risks and accept compliments.

What does "becoming human" mean to Amanda? "Becoming human" means accepting both our excellent qualities and excellent flaws. It is inherently human to mess up, and too often we aren't okay with that. To become human means to appreciate what we can do and be okay with not being perfect.
